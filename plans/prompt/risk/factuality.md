# Factuality Risks

## What are Factuality Risks?

Responses generated by Large Language Models (LLMs) may sound coherent and persuasive, but could be entirely fabricated. This phenomenon is called "hallucination" — producing information that appears authoritative but is actually inaccurate or misleading.

## Why Does This Matter?

When AI output looks professional and confident, users easily believe it, especially when they lack expertise in the domain. This can lead to misinformation propagation with serious consequences.

## How to Mitigate Factuality Risks?

### 1. Provide Ground Truth

Include relevant articles or reference materials as context in your prompts, giving the model correct information to rely on.

### 2. Configure Response Parameters

Lower `temperature` or `top-p` settings to reduce output randomness, making responses more stable and predictable.

### 3. Instruct to Admit Uncertainty

Directly tell the model to say "I don't know" or "I'm not sure" when lacking sufficient knowledge, rather than guessing.

### 4. Show Examples of Known vs. Unknown

Include examples in your prompt demonstrating which questions the model can answer correctly and which should be refused, establishing clear boundaries.

## Concrete Example

Here's a prompt example teaching the model to identify unknown information:

```
Please answer questions based on the facts below. If you know the answer, respond; if you don't know, respond with "?".

Known facts:
- Atoms are the basic units of chemical elements
- Mars has two moons: Phobos and Deimos

Please answer the following:
- "Who was the first president of the United States?"
- "What is this person's name: Neto Beto Roberto"
```

In this example, the model correctly answers the first question (if it knows) while responding "?" to the second question (the name is fabricated), demonstrating it has learned when to refuse answering.

## Key Takeaway

When providing prompts, combine examples showing questions the model may know and may not know to establish clear boundaries, helping the model learn when to admit ignorance rather than guessing randomly.
